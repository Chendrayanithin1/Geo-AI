# Competing Hypotheses Framework

Environmental observations rarely have a single obvious explanation.

The purpose of this framework is to encourage hypothesis generation rather than deterministic conclusions.

## Example Observation

Village: Anekal

NDVI = 0.22

Distance to nearest water body = 500 meters

## Hypothesis 1: Vegetation Stress

Description:

Vegetation stress occurs when vegetation health declines due to environmental pressures such as limited water availability, poor soil conditions, heat stress, or ecosystem degradation.

Supporting Evidence:

* Low NDVI
* Persistent decline over time

Contradicting Evidence:

* Strong water availability
* Stable vegetation trends

Confidence:

Medium

## Hypothesis 2: Urban Expansion

Description:

Urban growth often replaces vegetation with buildings, roads, and industrial infrastructure.

Supporting Evidence:

* Low NDVI
* Proximity to expanding urban regions
* Known development activity

Contradicting Evidence:

* Stable land use
* Protected green cover

Confidence:

Medium to High in rapidly urbanizing regions.

## Hypothesis 3: Land Use Change

Description:

Agricultural or natural land may be converted into industrial, residential, or commercial land.

Supporting Evidence:

* Declining vegetation cover
* Development corridors

Contradicting Evidence:

* No evidence of land conversion

Confidence:

Medium

## Hypothesis 4: Seasonal Effects

Description:

Vegetation indices naturally vary across seasons.

Supporting Evidence:

* Single observation date
* Agricultural region

Contradicting Evidence:

* Long-term vegetation decline

Confidence:

Low to Medium

## Hypothesis Ranking

The goal is not to select a single explanation immediately.

Instead:

1. Generate hypotheses.
2. Gather evidence.
3. Rank confidence.
4. Communicate uncertainty.

This process more closely reflects real-world environmental reasoning.
